Latent State Abstraction in Model-Based Reinforcement Learning: A Systematic Evaluation and Integration Strategy for TD-MPC2The optimization of model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL) algorithms in high-dimensional continuous control tasks is frequently bottlenecked by the structural capacity of the learned world model. While architectures such as Temporal-Difference Model Predictive Control (TD-MPC2) demonstrate high data efficiency by planning via Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) over short latent horizons, they remain vulnerable to environments characterized by sparse rewards, long-horizon temporal dependencies, and distracting visual observations.To overcome these boundaries, incorporating state and latent abstraction modules has emerged as a promising research direction. This report evaluates three primary abstraction families—Bisimulation/Behavioral State Abstractions, Temporal Abstractions, and Self-Predictive Representations—analyzing their mechanisms, empirical baselines, theoretical alignment with TD-MPC2, and failure modes.Architectural Baseline: The TD-MPC2 Representation ParadigmTo establish a baseline for comparative analysis, the core representation and training pipeline of TD-MPC2 must be formalized. TD-MPC2 learns an implicit, decoder-free latent world model consisting of five key components, all parameterized by neural networks utilizing LayerNorm and Mish activations :Encoder: $z_t = h(s_t, e)$ maps raw observations $s_t$ (and optional task embeddings $e$ in multitask settings) to a latent state $z_t$.Latent Dynamics: $z_{t+1} = d(z_t, a_t, e)$ predicts the next latent state given a continuous action $a_t$.Reward Predictor: $\hat{r}_t = R(z_t, a_t, e)$ predicts immediate scalar rewards.Value Approximator: $q_t = Q(z_t, a_t, e)$ estimates the expected cumulative return under a maximum entropy objective.Policy Prior: $a_t \sim \pi(z_t, e)$ outputs a continuous action prior to bootstrap the online planner.The latent space is regularized and stabilized using three primary training objectives :Latent Self-Consistency Loss: Forces the dynamics model to match the online encoder's output over a short sequence of transitions via a single-step consistency loss:$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{consistency}} = \|d(z_t, a_t, e) - \text{sg}(h(s_{t+1}, e))\|_2^2$$
where $\text{sg}$ is the stop-gradient operator.Discrete Regression Losses: Both reward and value targets are discretized into bins and optimized via cross-entropy rather than continuous mean squared error (MSE). This protects optimization from high variances in reward scale across tasks.SimNorm Regularization: Applies a group-wise softmax normalization to the latent representations $z_t$, partitioning the latent vector and projecting each sub-vector into a fixed-dimensional simplex. This enforces an active representation bottleneck, biasing the latent codes toward sparsity and preventing representational drift or gradient explosion without requiring target networks or pixel-reconstruction losses.At test-time, TD-MPC2 executes local trajectory optimization via MPPI over a short latent horizon (typically $H = 3$ to $5$ steps). Continuous action sequences are sampled, rolled out through the latent dynamics model $d$, evaluated using the reward and value heads, and updated iteratively.Comparative Survey of State Abstraction FamiliesBisimulation and Behavioral State Abstractions1. Abstraction MechanismBehavioral state abstractions seek to compress the observation space by grouping together states that are behaviorally equivalent. Under the mathematical framework of bisimulation, two states are equivalent if they yield the same immediate reward and transition to equivalent distributions of next states under any action.Architecturally, DeepMDP parameterizes this by training a latent transition model and a reward predictor to minimize next-state distribution and reward prediction errors. This optimization guarantees that states with divergent long-term rewards are not collapsed in the representation space.Deep Bisimulation for Control (DBC) relaxes this continuous metric in a practical neural network by training an encoder $\phi$ to ensure that the $\ell_1$ distance in latent space matches the bisimulation metric :
$$\|\phi(s_i) - \phi(s_j)\|_1 = |r(s_i, a) - r(s_j, a)| + \gamma W_1(\hat{\mathcal{P}}(\cdot|\phi(s_i), a), \hat{\mathcal{P}}(\cdot|\phi(s_j), a))$$
where $W_1$ represents the Wasserstein distance between the next-state latent distributions.Matching under Independent Couplings (MICo) avoids the heavy computation of Wasserstein distances by leveraging independent couplings of transition distributions to construct an on-policy metric. MICo approximates the behavioral distance $U_\omega(x, y)$ using a diffuse metric that admits non-zero self-distances, mapping states into an embedding space shaped by a target network copy:
$$U_\omega(x, y) := \frac{\|\phi_\omega(x)\|_2^2 + \|\phi_\omega(y)\|_2^2}{2} + \beta \theta(\phi_\omega(x), \phi_\omega(y))$$
where $\theta$ is the angle between the state representations.2. Theoretical IntuitionThis abstraction family directly addresses the vulnerability of pixel-reconstruction and task-agnostic contrastive methods. Standard autoencoders waste substantial model capacity attempting to reconstruct high-frequency background noise, moving distractors, or atmospheric effects. Enforcing bisimulation metric objectives restricts the latent representations to capture only factors of variation that influence immediate or expected cumulative rewards, effectively filtering out exogenous distractions.4. Fit-to-TD-MPC2 Integration AnalysisIn continuous control, TD-MPC2 already optimizes reward ($R$) and temporal-difference value ($Q$) heads alongside a single-step consistency loss. These task-specific losses implicitly act as behavioral constraints, structuring the latent space around reward-relevant dynamics.The primary theoretical gap in TD-MPC2 is that its consistency loss only constrains consecutive states along a trajectory ($z_t \to z_{t+1}$). It does not regulate the pairwise distances of arbitrary states in the replay buffer.However, integrating a true bisimulation metric (such as DBC or MICo) on top of TD-MPC2 introduces severe computational bottlenecks. Computing pairwise distances across a standard batch of transitions requires evaluating $O(B^2)$ operations (where $B$ is the batch size), which is mathematically incompatible with a compute-matched evaluation protocol.5. Negative Results and Known Failure ModesBehavioral metrics are highly sensitive to policy non-stationarity. Because the distance metric is calculated relative to the rewards and transitions observed under the current policy (as in the on-policy variant of MICo), any updates to the policy immediately alter the ground-truth bisimulation targets. This introduces severe target non-stationarity into the representation learning objective, which can destabilize the encoder and degrade convergence rates in online settings.Additionally, standard bisimulation requires exact matching of actions, which is brittle in high-dimensional continuous action spaces; even lax bisimulation mappings struggle to scale when continuous action spaces exhibit multi-modal symmetries.Temporal Abstraction, Hierarchical Models, and Skills1. Abstraction MechanismTemporal abstraction models decision-making over extended timescales by structuring behaviors into hierarchical policies.Architecturally, Director achieves this by splitting control between two neural networks optimized within a learned latent world model :Manager Policy: Operates at a coarse temporal resolution, choosing an abstract latent subgoal $z$ from a factorized discrete categorical space ($8\times 8$ categoricals) every fixed $K=8$ environmental steps.Goal Autoencoder: Compresses continuous $1024$-dimensional latent states into these discrete codes to prevent the manager from proposing out-of-distribution or unreachable regions. The discrete goal is decoded back into the latent feature space as $g = \text{dec}(z)$.Worker Policy: Operates at a fine temporal resolution, taking the current latent state $s_t$ and the manager's decoded goal $g$ to output low-level continuous actions $a_t$. The worker is trained via imagined world model rollouts to maximize a goal-similarity reward (e.g., cosine similarity to $g$).The manager's reward is a linear combination of the external task reward and an intrinsic exploration reward defined by the reconstruction error of the goal autoencoder, encouraging exploration of poorly modeled states.2. Theoretical IntuitionFlat planners (including TD-MPC2’s MPPI search) suffer from compounding latent drift and exponential search-space scaling as the planning horizon increases. By executing a multi-step temporal abstraction, the Manager routes the agent across distant latent checkpoints. The Worker is then left with a highly localized, dense continuous control task. This bypasses the need for the model to accurately predict fine-grained action sequences over thousands of steps, shielding the policy from compounding dynamics errors.4. Fit-to-TD-MPC2 Integration AnalysisVanilla TD-MPC2 operates strictly on a flat, single-step timescale with a short planning horizon ($H = 3$ to $5$). It possesses no mechanism for temporal hierarchy, rendering it incapable of solving sparse-reward visual navigation tasks (such as Ant Maze) without dense reward shaping.There is an exceptional architectural alignment between the two frameworks: TD-MPC2’s stable, SimNorm-regularized latent space can act as the underlying representation.The primary gap is that TD-MPC2’s MPPI planner is local and short-sighted. By placing a Director-style discrete latent goal framework on top of TD-MPC2, the MPPI planner can be repurposed as a goal-conditioned worker. Instead of maximizing predicted external rewards over a short horizon, the MPPI planner optimizes the action sequence to maximize latent cosine similarity to the high-level manager's subgoal. This addresses the long-horizon planning limitation of TD-MPC2 without modifying its core continuous control mechanisms.5. Negative Results and Known Failure ModesHierarchical control introduces severe optimization non-stationarity. If the worker policy is updated concurrently but has not yet learned to reach subgoals, the manager receives noisy, uninformative rewards, which can destabilize the high-level policy.In dense-reward continuous locomotion tasks (such as Hopper, Walker, and HalfCheetah), temporal hierarchy actually degrades performance. Forcing the worker to pursue a static subgoal over a fixed $K$-step interval prevents the continuous adjustments needed for fluid, energy-efficient locomotion, leading to sub-optimal gaits and lower asymptotic performance than flat models.Self-Predictive and Contrastive Representations1. Abstraction MechanismSelf-predictive representation learning focuses on predicting future abstract latent states rather than reconstructing raw visual observations.Architecturally, Self-Predictive Representations (SPR) trains a dual-network configuration :Online Network: Learns to project the current observation into a latent state $z_t$, which is rolled forward $K$ steps using a latent transition model and continuous actions.Target Network: Updates its weights $\theta_m$ as an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of the online network's weights $\theta_o$:$$\theta_m \leftarrow \tau\theta_m + (1-\tau)\theta_o$$
A strict stop-gradient operator is applied to the target outputs to prevent representational collapse.The online predictions and target representations are projected into a lower-dimensional space, and the loss minimizes the negative cosine similarity over a multi-step future horizon :
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{SPR}} = -\sum_{k=1}^K \frac{\tilde{y}_{t+k}}{\|\tilde{y}_{t+k}\|_2} \cdot \frac{\hat{y}_{t+k}}{\|\hat{y}_{t+k}\|_2}$$
where $\hat{y}_{t+k}$ is the projected online prediction and $\tilde{y}_{t+k}$ is the projected target representation. Data augmentation (such as random shifts) is applied to the online input to force representational consistency across multiple visual views.BYOL-Explore utilizes a similar multi-step predictive loss over an EMA target network, but uses the prediction error directly as an intrinsic curiosity reward to drive exploration.2. Theoretical IntuitionBy utilizing a self-supervised bootstrap objective, SPR forces the latent space to prioritize temporally consistent, action-controllable dynamics. Since it does not rely on a decoder, the world model ignores high-frequency, complex background textures that cannot be predicted across time under the agent’s actions.The EMA target network and the stop-gradient operator provide a stable, slowly changing target for the representation space, preventing representational collapse without restricting latent capacity.4. Fit-to-TD-MPC2 Integration AnalysisTD-MPC2 already implements a self-predictive consistency objective. Its latent consistency loss aligns the predicted next latent state $d(z_t, a_t)$ with the online encoder’s output $h(s_{t+1})$.The primary differences are:TD-MPC2’s consistency loss is strictly single-step ($K=1$), whereas SPR enforces multi-step consistency up to $K=5$ steps.TD-MPC2 completely avoids the use of an EMA target network and stop-gradients on the target state, relying instead on SimNorm to prevent representational collapse.Integrating a full multi-step SPR objective would introduce substantial redundancy. Moreover, introducing an EMA target network with a target smoothing rate $\tau$ would add a highly sensitive hyperparameter. This runs counter to TD-MPC2’s core design principle of maintaining a single set of robust hyperparameters across all tasks.5. Negative Results and Known Failure ModesSelf-predictive bootstrap architectures are vulnerable to "quadratic collapse" if optimized via standard $L_2$ distance without normalization. Cosine similarity is mandatory to maintain representation stability.Additionally, because these methods prioritize predictability, they can actively discard highly task-relevant stochastic features. If an environment contains a random, uncontrollable hazard (e.g., a visual distractor that occasionally blocks a path randomly), the self-predictive loss will map this stochastic element to noise, hiding it from the planner and causing planning failures.Strategic Decision Analysis1. Optimal Abstraction Mechanism for TD-MPC2The single abstraction mechanism most likely to deliver a real, architecture-level performance gain over vanilla TD-MPC2 is Temporal Abstraction via a Hierarchical Discrete Latent Goal Framework (conceptually derived from Director ).Theoretical JustificationTD-MPC2 already possesses a highly optimized representation learning pipeline. Its joint training of reward and value prediction heads, combined with a single-step latent self-consistency loss and SimNorm normalization, successfully extracts task-relevant, collapse-free representations without the need for pixel reconstruction. Consequently, adding behavioral metric constraints (DBC/MICo) or self-predictive targets (SPR) introduces severe redundancy and significant computational overhead.The fundamental bottleneck of TD-MPC2 is horizon blindness. Because local MPPI trajectory optimization is executed over a short horizon ($H = 3$ to $5$), the agent is structurally incapable of solving long-horizon, sparse-reward tasks (such as Ant Maze, Crafter, or sequential robotic manipulation). Simply expanding the planning horizon of the flat MPPI planner is computationally prohibitive and causes planning to fail due to compounding dynamics errors.A hierarchical discrete latent goal framework directly addresses this limitation. By utilizing a goal autoencoder to discretize and constrain the latent state space into factorized categorical codes, a high-level manager policy can plan at a coarse temporal resolution (e.g., $K=8$ steps). The low-level TD-MPC2 MPPI planner acts as the goal-conditioned worker, optimizing actions over its short, stable horizon to maximize latent similarity to the manager's subgoal. This provides a clean separation of concerns: the manager routes the agent across long-term milestones, while the low-level MPPI planner resolves continuous control and high-frequency contacts.2. Standard Fair Evaluation ProtocolTo isolate the true value of latent abstractions and prevent "exploration luck" or hyperparameter overfitting from polluting results, the evaluation must adhere to a standardized, rigorous protocol.The evaluation suite must be designed to isolate specific abstraction properties:Visual Distractor DeepMind Control (DMC) Suite: Isolates behavioral and state abstraction. The background is replaced with complex natural video distractors, testing the agent’s capacity to filter out exogenous visual noise without relying on pixel reconstruction.Crafter / Egocentric Ant Maze: Isolates temporal abstraction and long-horizon credit assignment. These environments feature extremely sparse, delayed rewards where flat planning baselines are mathematically incapable of finding the goal through local search.Statistical FrameworkStandard evaluations relying on the best-reached reward or sample means over 3 to 5 seeds are highly unreliable and dominated by statistical uncertainty. The evaluation must utilize the rliable library to compute and report :Interquartile Mean (IQM): The mean performance over the middle 50% of runs combined across all tasks and seeds. IQM is highly robust to outlier seeds (such as a single seed entering a bimodal gaits basin on HopperHop) while remaining far more statistically efficient than the median.Stratified Bootstrap Confidence Intervals: 95% confidence intervals generated via $N=50,000$ bootstrap resamples to quantify statistical significance.Probability of Improvement (PI): The probability that the abstraction architecture outperforms the vanilla baseline on a randomly selected task and seed run.Performance Profiles: Curves showing the distribution of scores across all tasks and runs to reveal the tail behavior of the algorithm.3. Clean Minimal Validation ExperimentTo prove that state/temporal abstraction provides a genuine architectural advantage over vanilla TD-MPC2—without relying on training-procedure tricks—the following experiment is proposed:Environment SetupTask: Egocentric Ant Maze Medium (proprioception + egocentric camera, sparse terminal reward).Baseline: Vanilla TD-MPC2 with planning horizons $H \in \{3, 5, 10\}$.Proposed Agent: Hierarchical TD-MPC2 (H-TD-MPC2).Agent ConfigurationsH-TD-MPC2: A Manager policy selects a discrete latent goal $z \in \mathcal{Z}$ (where $\mathcal{Z}$ is discretized via a goal autoencoder trained online) every $K=8$ environmental steps. The low-level TD-MPC2 agent utilizes its standard MPPI planner to optimize continuous actions over a short horizon $H=3$. The MPPI objective is modified to maximize the cosine similarity between the predicted latent state and the manager’s subgoal in the latent space.Hyperparameter Matching: Both agents are trained for exactly 1 million environmental interaction steps. The batch size, network sizes, and learning rates are kept identical. The computational budget (FLOPs per step) is matched by restricting the low-level MPPI search to $H=3$ in H-TD-MPC2, while the high-level manager runs only once every 8 steps. All procedural tricks (such as population-based training, scheduling, and resets) are disabled.Statistical Coverage: Run 10 independent random seeds for both configurations.Expected Results and Reviewer ValidationBecause Egocentric Ant Maze Medium requires navigating a complex visual maze under sparse reward feedback, vanilla TD-MPC2 will fail entirely, collapsing to a 0% success rate across all evaluated planning horizons. Expanding the flat planning horizon to $H=10$ or $15$ will not help, as compounding dynamics errors and the exponential expansion of the MPPI continuous action search space will prevent the planner from discovering the distant goal.In contrast, H-TD-MPC2 will exhibit a step-function performance win (reaching a high success rate on IQM profiles), as the Manager successfully routes the agent across latent checkpoints, while the low-level MPPI planner focuses on localized continuous movement. This stark, bimodal separation of performance on a sparse-reward task provides an undeniable, structurally validated proof of abstraction value that cannot be attributed to statistical luck or seed selection.
